# Why the best leaders let themselves be seen

This is not my writing. It is a pointer to Brené Brown's Dare to Lead, with a few words on why it matters to me.

Her central argument is that you cannot build trust on a team without vulnerability. Not oversharing, but the everyday courage to say "I don't know", "I got that wrong", or "I need help". Brown's research shows that the leaders people actually want to follow are the ones who are willing to be seen, not the ones performing certainty.

This took me a while to believe. Early on I thought a manager was supposed to have the answers, and admitting otherwise felt risky. What I found in practice was the opposite. The moment I started being honest about what I did not know, the team relaxed and started being honest too. Problems surfaced earlier. People stopped hiding mistakes until they became expensive.

It connects to something I care about a lot: I have ADHD and I am open about it. Being upfront about how I work, what I am brilliant at and where I struggle, is the same muscle Brown is talking about. It costs a little pride and buys a lot of trust.

If you manage people, it is worth your time. Read the original at the link above.
