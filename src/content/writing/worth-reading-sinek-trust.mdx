# Why leaders eat last, and why it stuck with me

This is not my writing. It is a pointer to Simon Sinek's work, with a few words on why it shaped how I lead.

The core idea is simple. The job of a leader is not to be the smartest person in the room or to have every answer. It is to create an environment where people feel safe enough to do their best work, take risks, and own their mistakes. Sinek frames it around the military idea that leaders eat last, that you look after your people before yourself.

I came to this after years of managing technical teams, and it put words to something I already half believed. The best teams I have worked on were not the ones with the most talented individuals. They were the ones where people trusted each other enough to say "I don't know" or "I got that wrong" without bracing for it. That trust does not appear on its own. Someone has to go first, and that someone is usually whoever is leading.

The part I keep coming back to is that this is a daily practice, not a personality trait. You build that safety in small moments: how you react when something breaks, whether you take the blame upward and the credit downward, whether you actually back people when it counts. Get those right often enough and you get a team that runs itself. Get them wrong and no amount of process will save you.

If you lead people, it is worth your time. Read the original at the link above.
