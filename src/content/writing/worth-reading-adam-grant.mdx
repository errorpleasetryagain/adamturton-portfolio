# The case for rethinking what you know

This is not my writing. It is a pointer to Adam Grant's Think Again, with a few words on why it stuck with me.

The core idea is that being smart is less useful than being willing to rethink. Grant argues that we treat our opinions like possessions to defend rather than ideas to test, and that the best thinkers hold their views loosely enough to update them when the evidence changes.

I came to this from a slightly different angle. I have ADHD, which means I question things almost by default, sometimes to a fault. What Grant gave me was a frame for treating that as a strength rather than a flaw. In tech operations you are constantly told "this is how we do it here", and a lot of the time that is exactly the thing worth re-examining. Some of the best improvements I have made came from asking why a process existed at all, not from following it better.

The part that matters for leading people is that you have to make it safe for the team to rethink too. If changing your mind looks like weakness, nobody will do it, and you end up defending bad decisions out of pride. Get that culture right and the team gets smarter over time on its own.

Worth your time if you lead anything. Read the original at the link above.
